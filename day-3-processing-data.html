A relational database reduces data redundancy by storing customer, order and inventory data in separate linked tables rather than repeating information across every row of one spreadsheet. This makes updates more reliable (e.g. changing a customer's address once, not everywhere it appears) and allows the business to scale to thousands of orders without slowing down or corrupting data.

Model Answer (Band 6 standard)
As order volume grows into the thousands, a single spreadsheet becomes increasingly prone to data duplication (e.g. repeating customer details on every order row), slower performance, and a higher risk of human error when manually updating records. A relational database addresses this by storing customers, orders and inventory in separate linked tables, maintaining data integrity through defined relationships and allowing efficient querying even at large scale. While migration involves upfront cost and a learning curve, the long-term gains in accuracy, scalability and reduced redundancy make it a worthwhile investment for a business growing at this rate.
Marking criteria:
  • 5 — Makes an informed judgement about migration, supported by comprehensive reference to data integrity, duplication and scalability
  • 3-4 — Makes a judgement about migration, supported by reference to at least two of: integrity, duplication, scalability
  • 2 — Describes a limitation of spreadsheets or a benefit of relational databases
