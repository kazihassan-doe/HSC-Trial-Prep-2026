Model Answer
Raw data is often unclean, unsorted, and may contain errors, duplicates or irrelevant entries. This makes patterns hard to see and can lead to poor decisions if acted on directly. For example, a retailer using unfiltered sales logs might miscalculate stock needs because returns and cancelled orders weren't removed. Processing is needed before the data is reliable for decision-making.

Active data collection involves directly asking users for information, such as a customer completing a satisfaction survey. Passive data collection happens automatically in the background, such as a website recording click patterns via cookies. The key difference is the level of user awareness and effort involved in providing the data.

Model Answer
If a sample is not representative of the wider population, the data collected will be biased and conclusions drawn from it may not reflect reality. For example, surveying only customers who visit during weekday mornings excludes working customers, skewing results toward retirees or parents. A properly randomised or stratified sample reduces this risk and increases reliability.
Marking criteria:

  • 2 — Outlines how sampling method may affect the reliability of data
  • 1 — Provides some relevant information

Model Answer
Primary data is collected first-hand by the enterprise for a specific purpose (e.g. its own customer survey), while secondary data is collected by someone else for a different purpose and reused (e.g. ABS census data). This matters for reliability because primary data is usually more directly relevant and current, while secondary data may be outdated or not perfectly suited to the enterprise's context, even though it can be faster and cheaper to obtain.
Marking criteria:
  • 3 — Explains the difference between primary and secondary data and why this matters for reliability
  • 2 — Describes primary and/or secondary data OR outlines a reliability consideration

Model Answer (Band 6 standard)
Likes and emoticons offer enterprises fast, large-volume, low-cost feedback, useful for gauging broad sentiment on a product launch in near real-time. However, this data has significant reliability limitations: it lacks context (a "like" doesn't explain why), is prone to bias toward extreme reactions, can be manipulated by bots, and reflects only users who chose to engage. Because it cannot be verified for accuracy or intent, it is best treated as a supplementary indicator rather than a reliable primary data source — enterprises should combine it with structured feedback to draw valid conclusions.
